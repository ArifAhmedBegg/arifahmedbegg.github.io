About Me

I am a second-year M.Sc. student in Chemistry at the Indian Institute of Technology Kharagpur. My academic interests lie at the intersection of computational quantum chemistry, spin physics, and molecular magnetism. My work focuses on theoretical and computational approaches to understand the magnetic and spectroscopic properties of molecular systems, particularly those relevant to quantum information science.

Currently, my Master's thesis research investigates TEMPO-based nitroxide radicals as potential molecular qubits. Using quantum chemical calculations and computational spectroscopy tools such as ORCA and EasySpin, I analyse electronic structures, EPR parameters (g and hyperfine tensors), vibrational contributions, spin–phonon coupling, and relaxation mechanisms that influence coherence in molecular spin systems.

I have gained research experience through academic projects and internships, including work on modelling quantum spin systems and studying adsorption kinetics of organic dyes on activated carbon materials. My long-term research goal is to contribute to the development of molecular systems for quantum technologies and advanced magnetic materials.

Projects

Spin Hamiltonian Simulations

Development of computational models for interacting spin systems using spin Hamiltonians. This project involves simulating dipolar interactions, analysing spin–spin coupling, and studying how spatial arrangement and orientation of spins influence magnetic behaviour. The simulations are implemented in Python to visualise the angular dependence of dipolar coupling and to understand the fundamental physics governing molecular spin systems.

EPR Spectroscopy and TEMPO-Based Molecular Qubits

Computational investigation of nitroxide radicals, particularly TEMPO-based molecules, as potential molecular qubits. The project aims to understand their electronic structure and magnetic properties through quantum chemical calculations and EPR spectrum simulations. Using tools such as ORCA and EasySpin, the work focuses on evaluating g-tensors, hyperfine interactions, and spin–spin coupling effects, with the broader goal of understanding how such systems can be utilised in quantum information and molecular spin-based technologies.

Solvent Dependent Charge Transfer and Acidity in p-Nitroaniline

A theoretical study of solvent-dependent charge transfer in p-Nitroaniline highlights how predictions vary strongly with the choice of density functional in Density Functional Theory. Range-separated and hybrid functionals better capture long-range charge-transfer excitations, while local functionals tend to underestimate excitation energies and over-delocalize electron density. This functional dependence directly influences computed acidity (pKa) trends across solvents, underscoring the need for careful method selection in modelling solvent effects and electronic properties.
